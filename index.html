Overlook Sleep at Your Peril

If you'd like to significantly increase the risk of cancer, cardiovascular diseases, neurodegenerative diseases, and mental health disorders, then not getting enough sleep, i.e., 7-8 hours, or just good sleep, is a great strategy.

Consequences of Poor Sleep and Lack of Sleep
Effects on Society
  • Car Crashes: Sleep deprivation impairs reaction times and decision-making, contributing to thousands of car accidents annually. Studies estimate that drowsy driving causes up to 20% of fatal crashes in some regions.
  • Workplace Accidents: Fatigue from lack of sleep increases errors and injuries in high-risk jobs like construction, healthcare, and manufacturing, costing billions in damages and lost productivity.
  • Healthcare Burden: Chronic sleep deprivation drives up rates of obesity, diabetes, and heart disease, straining public health systems and increasing medical costs.
  • Social Tension: Irritability and poor emotional regulation from sleep loss can strain relationships, increase conflict, and reduce community cohesion.
Effects on the Immune System
  • Reduced Immune Response: Sleep is critical for producing cytokines, proteins that fight infection and inflammation. Less sleep means fewer cytokines, weakening your defense against viruses and bacteria.
  • Slower Wound Healing: Lack of sleep impairs the body's ability to repair itself, delaying recovery from injuries or infections.
  • Increased Inflammation: Chronic sleep loss triggers higher levels of inflammatory markers (e.g., C-reactive protein), which can contribute to diseases like arthritis or diabetes.
  • Higher Infection Risk: With a weakened immune system, you're more susceptible to colds, flu, and other illnesses.
Effects on Hormones
  • Testosterone: In men, sleep loss lowers testosterone levels, affecting muscle mass, libido, and energy. A single night of bad sleep can drop it by 10-15%.
  • Cortisol (Stress Hormone): Sleep deprivation spikes cortisol levels, keeping you in a stressed, fight-or-flight state. This can lead to anxiety and burnout.
  • Growth Hormone: Released during deep sleep, this hormone aids growth in kids and repair in adults. Lack of sleep cuts its production, slowing recovery and aging the body faster.
  • Insulin: Poor sleep reduces insulin sensitivity, raising blood sugar levels and increasing the risk of type 2 diabetes.
  • Thyroid Hormones: Chronic sleep deprivation can suppress thyroid function, slowing metabolism and energy production.
Effects on the Body
  • Cognitive Impairment: Sleep deprivation reduces focus, memory, and decision-making ability. Reaction times slow, mimicking the effects of alcohol intoxication.
  • Weight Gain: Lack of sleep disrupts appetite regulation, increasing cravings for high-calorie, sugary foods. It also slows metabolism.
  • Muscle and Tissue Repair: Sleep is when the body repairs muscles, tissues, and cells. Without it, recovery slows, leading to fatigue, weakness, and sometimes, serious injury.
  • Increased Pain Sensitivity: Poor sleep lowers pain tolerance, making aches and chronic pain feel worse.
  • Digestive Issues: Sleep deprivation can disrupt gut health, leading to problems like acid reflux or irregular bowel movements.
How to Get Better Sleep
Time
  • Consistency is king. Try going to bed at the same time each night. Try getting up at the same time each morning.
  • Your body will start to naturally combat insomnia by starting to crave sleep at your bedtime.
Light and Color
  • Try to avoid bright lights starting at least one hour before going to bed.
  • Try to avoid blue light during this time as well because it will stimulate you and make it harder to fall asleep.
Eating and Drinking Before Bedtime
  • Try to stop eating and drinking at least one hour before going to bed.
  • This will let your body focus on rest and relaxation — instead of digestion — which will improve sleep quality.
  • This will also help reduce waking up to pee and interrupting sleep duration and quality.
Caffeine and Alcohol Before Bedtime
  • Alcohol reduces REM (rapid eye movement) sleep, the restorative phase critical for memory and mood regulation.
  • Alcohol relaxes throat muscles, worsening snoring or obstructive sleep apnea, which can cause repeated awakenings and oxygen deprivation during the night.
  • Caffeine blocks adenosine receptors, a chemical that promotes sleepiness, making it harder to fall asleep and potentially delaying sleep by 30-60 minutes or more.
  • Even if you fall asleep, caffeine decreases deep sleep (slow-wave sleep), leaving you feeling less rested and more fatigued the next day.
  • Try to stop drinking alcohol at least two hours before going to bed. Try to stop drinking caffeine at least six hours before going to bed.
Energy State Before Bedtime
  • Try to avoid jacking up your energy state starting at least one hour before going to bed. Otherwise, going to sleep will be far more challenging.
  • Try to focus on activities that help calm you down and relax you.
Temperature
  • Try to avoid overly warm conditions if possible because they will stimulate you and make it harder to fall asleep.
  • Try to create a cooler environment around you before going to bed. Try to maintain this cooler environment while sleeping to maximize sleep quality. Air conditioning can be very helpful here but also extremely expensive. A great alternative is to use a fan. Fans can be just as effective as AC while also being significantly less expensive and more energy efficient.
  • AC or even fans may not be working for you. Try drinking a big, cold glass of water with lots of ice. Yes, you may wake up in the middle of the night to pee, but that's not nearly as bad as not falling asleep in the first place.
Sound
  • Try to avoid lots of loud or chaotic noises that stimulate you at least one hour before going to bed.
  • Some people prefer little to no noise to maintain sleep quality. Some people prefer white noise. Find what works for you.
Jaw Relaxation
  • Clenching of the jaw is a common contributor to insomnia.
  • Try to catch yourself tensing up any muscles in your body — especially your jaw — and try to relax them to induce a powerful calming effect.
Overthinking and Ruminating
  • Overthinking and ruminating are probably the biggest drivers of insomnia.
  • If you can't fall asleep within 15-30 minutes of going to bed, are you overthinking or ruminating?
  • If so, try to stop and think about your happy place, or nothing at all. The latter is surprisingly effective, but challenging to do.
  • If those don't work, have a notepad nearby that you can use to write down your worries and frustrations. Try to capture in words what's really bothering you.
  • Overthinking and ruminating are your brain's ways of trying to help you. Your brain wants you to see and fully understand some specific problems, and then solve them to avoid future pain. Show it some respect. Write it all down. Writing proves to your brain that you actually see the problems and threats, which is half the battle. Once you prove this to your brain through writing, it will then allow you to relax and fall asleep.
  • But before trying to fall asleep again, schedule a time tomorrow to further tackle your thoughts and worries. The goal is to fully prove to your brain that you see these threats and are committed to tackling them. Tell your brain you need sleep to do so, or these future problems could get much worse.

Exercise over Diet.

The adage, "You can't outrun a bad diet," is mostly true. However, being sedentary is catastrophic to our health because it significantly increases the risk of death from cardiovascular disease and cancer. Furthermore, this adage fails to account for the benefits exercise has on sticking to a healthier diet. Embarking on a healthy diet without exercise can be quite challenging. But exercise can make it much easier by improving mental health, elevating nutrient absorption, and dampening food cravings, especially for unhealthy food.

Exercise is Spring Cleaning for Your Body and Mind.

Every time you exercise, your body goes into action removing old, damaged cells to make way for new, healthy ones. And intense exercise increases body temperature, metabolic stress, and oxidative stress. These stressors trigger the release of heat shock proteins, which help protect cells by refolding damaged proteins, preventing protein clumping, and aiding cellular repair.

Therefore, Exercise is a whole body and mind rejuvenation.

Without exercise, your body becomes clogged and cluttered with unhealthy cells, which significantly increases the risk of disease and cancer.

So remember, movement is medicine.

Injury

Try to avoid getting injured or you won't be able to maintain crucial consistency. Showing up for even a little bit of exercise is infinitely better than not.

The leading causes of injury tend to be:

  • Explosive actions. Moving too fast.
  • Lifting much heavier weight than your body is used to, or doing many more sets/reps than your body is used to.
  • Poor technique and form.
  • Not warming up the muscles, tendons, ligaments, and joints before intense effort.
  • Pushing through pain that feels like a sudden, sharp sting. A burning sensation is generally what you're looking for. Sudden sharp pain is not.
  • If your target muscles or joints for the day are still really sore, and you can't lift around the same weight, reps, and sets you did last time, you probably need more time off from the gym — or at least lighter weight per rep — to help give them time to heal and grow stronger.
Strength Training

Add strength training to your routine to build muscle strength, improve bone health, boost metabolism, and manage weight. And look more attractive!
Muscles are huge blood sugar (glucose) sinks, which increases insulin sensitivity and lowers the risk of type 2 diabetes and dementia. In fact, skeletal muscle is the largest site of glucose disposal in the entire body.
Aim for at least one 30-minute workout per week, and 6 sets per muscle group (e.g. chest).

Muscles and bones are challenged at the gym through iron and sweat.
Muscles and bones are built at home through food and sleep.

Your #1 job is to tell your body that this muscle needs to grow.
You do that by putting stress on your muscles, joints, organs, and body. As David Goggins put it, "To grow, you must suffer."
One interesting side effect of exercise stress is that some cancer cells are too vulnerable to handle it. They die — not all of them — but many do because of their vulnerable state. Your body rebuilds and replenishes your healthy cells as a response to exercise stress, but some cancer cells will die under these stressful conditions.

The time of day you strength train matters. For me, I'm at least 20-33% weaker in the early mornings. I'm much stronger and more driven around lunchtime. That time isn't doable for everyone. Find what works for you. But the most important thing is that you do strength train, period.

The main question you should be asking yourself is, "Did I get the sets in for my muscles this week?" That means at least 6 sets for that muscle group (e.g. chest) to satisfy consistency for this week.

Workout Routines

Muscle splits, muscle groups, and exercises.
Isolation exercises vs. Compound exercises. Isolation exercises provide a bigger stimulus to the target muscle and better results but now workouts take twice as long. Find a balance that works with your schedule.
Sets: warm-up, working.
Reps: full range of motion, lengthened partials, myoreps, and isometric holds.
Reps in reserve (RIR): The number of perfect technique reps you could still do after you end a set. If you start struggling and your technique falters, you just found your 0 RIR.
Dropsets and Supersets.

Example Workouts
Regular Workout, ~35 Minutes
Warm-up jog: 1-5 minutes. Having an elaborate 15 to 20-minute warm-up is penny smart, dollar stupid. You're here to strength train. Make it so.
Rest between sets: 30 seconds to 2 minutes.

Bicep Curl, Standing or Seated Incline Bench, Dumbbells
  • 1 warm-up set, 12–20 reps with light weight
  • 6 working sets, 8–12 reps, 0-2 reps in reserve (RIR)
Incline Chest Press, Dumbbells or Machine
  • 1 warm-up set, 12–20 reps with light weight
  • 6 working sets, 8–12 reps, 0-2 reps in reserve (RIR)
Shoulder Side Lateral Raise, Dumbbells, or Abdominal Hanging Leg Raise
  • 1 warm-up set, 12–20 reps with light weight
  • 6 working sets, 8–12 reps, 0-2 reps in reserve (RIR)

Now I don't like doing all 6 working sets at a time for a single muscle. I prefer smaller victories and splitting the workout in half to create two cycles. For example:

  • Bicep Curl: 1 warm-up set, 3 working sets
  • Incline Chest Press: 1 warm-up set, 3 working sets
  • Shoulder Side Lateral Raise: 1 warm-up set, 3 working sets

Then do this entire cycle all over again minus the warm-up sets.
I love the small victories of finishing each muscle exercise this split provides. And by splitting the workout this way, I get that feeling twice as much for every workout.

Advanced Workout, ~35 Minutes
This workout is only available after you have at least 8 months of consistent regular or light workouts under your belt.
Why? Because you need 8 months to strengthen your muscles, tendons, ligaments, and joints before you are ready. If you start this advanced workout without doing so, you will significantly increase the chances of getting injured.

  • 1 warm-up set, 12–20 reps with light weight
  • 4 working sets, 8–12 reps, 0-2 RIR
  • 2 working sets, 5-6 reps, 0-1 RIR, and at the end of the last rep for both sets: isometric hold for 5-10 seconds or 3-5 lengthened partial reps
Seated Row, Machine, or Standing Bent-Over Row, Dumbbells
  • 1 warm-up set, 12–20 reps with light weight

  • 2 working sets, 5–6 reps, 0-1 RIR, and at the end of the last rep for both sets: isometric hold for 5-10 seconds or 3-5 lengthened partial reps

  • 2 working sets, 5–6 reps, 0-1 RIR, and at the end of the last rep for both sets: isometric hold for 5-10 seconds or 3-5 lengthened partial reps

Sugar and Refined Carbohydrates

Probably the deadliest nutrients if consumed in excess are sugars and refined carbohydrates because they are mostly stripped of their fiber, vitamins, and minerals. They also cause immediate and huge spikes in blood sugar (glucose), which can lead to insulin resistance and eventually diabetes.
Sugars and refined carbs are also incredibly addictive and increase hunger instead of satisfying it. This catastrophic combination makes it far easier for us to overconsume calories we don't need, especially unhealthy calories that drive disease and cancer.

Overconsuming calories — especially from sugars and refined carbohydrates — is one of the leading killers of humans on Earth because it's a major driver of cardiovascular disease (the #1 global killer), type 2 diabetes, obesity-related cancers, and non-alcoholic fatty liver disease. Dr. Frank Hu of Harvard revealed that people who consume 17–21% of their calories from added sugar had a 38% higher risk of dying from heart disease compared to those consuming less than 10%.

Therefore, in general, we can dramatically improve our health by eating fewer calories, refined carbs, sugar, and saturated fats, and eating more protein, whole carbs, fiber, and unsaturated fats (discussed in detail below).

Fasting Plus Big Meals - Pros and Cons:
  • Improves metabolic health and insulin sensitivity.
  • The chief benefit of fasting is likely not fasting itself, but simply reducing overall caloric intake.
  • Can reduce muscle mass due to insufficient protein intake, which increases the risk of deadly falls and accidents.
  • Decreases energy and mood by increasing anxiety, depression, lethargy, and irritability. On average, hungry people tend to be angry people. Hence the widespread use of the word "Hangry."
  • A common question that clinical psychologists will ask a patient who comes in for anxiety and depression is, "What are you eating for breakfast?" If you don't eat in the morning and break your fast (aka breakfast), your body may significantly boost the stress hormone cortisol because you're body is hungry and needs nutrition. This spike in cortisol above normal levels can exacerbate symptoms of anxiety and depression.
  • Creates huge spikes in blood sugar (glucose) when you eat, especially if the meal is largely refined carbs and sugar. This can result in crashes in energy and mental clarity.
More Regular Small Meals - Pros and Cons:
  • Improves metabolic health and insulin sensitivity.
  • Provides sustained energy and mental acuity throughout the day, and without big crashes.
  • Dampens hunger signals because you know you'll be eating again in ~3 hours anyway.
  • Improves sleep quality.
  • Small meals can become big meals if you're not careful and deliberate.
  • Can be inconvenient or embarrassing to eat at certain times, like when no one else is eating but you.
Hydration
Main Consequences of Dehydration:
  • Muscle cramps, fatigue, and worse physical performance.
  • Dry, wrinkly skin on top of an increased heart attack risk from elevated blood pressure and thicker blood.
  • Worse mood, mental clarity, and problem solving skills, i.e., you're literally dumber.
  • Tooth decay, kidney stones, urinary tract infections, and digestion issues.
  • Overeating and weight gain. Thirst can often feel like hunger.
Main Causes of Dehydration:
  • Not drinking enough water.
  • Excessive fluid loss from diuretics (caffeine, alcohol, others), sweating, fever, etc.
  • Not consuming enough electrolytes (potassium, sodium, magnesium). However, be careful with sodium. The average American is already consuming way too much sodium, which raises blood pressure, damages organs, and can lead to a stroke.
  • Vomiting or diarrhea.
  • Underlying health problems (diabetes, kidney diseases, burns, etc.).
You're Dehydrated if:
  • Your urine is yellow and not mostly clear.
  • Your skin or lips feel dry. Using lotion or chapstick is fantastic, but these do not address the underlying dehydration problem.
The Leading Causes of Death (In Ranked Order):

1. Cardiovascular Diseases (Coronary Artery Disease, Stroke, Heart Failure, etc.)
Top 5 Causes:
  1. Poor Diet
    • Diets high in calories, refined carbohydrates, sugar, saturated fats, and sodium raise blood pressure, cholesterol, triglycerides, and blood sugar (i.e., glucose, or A1C if measuring the average level of glucose over the past two to three months).
    • There are several large, well-designed randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and meta-analyses that provide strong evidence that excessive sodium intake significantly increases blood pressure.
    • High levels of LDL cholesterol and triglycerides contribute to plaque formation in arteries and hardening of the arteries, which lead to blockages, heart attacks, and heart disease.
    • High levels of blood sugar (glucose) damage blood vessels and organs through inflammation, oxidative stress, and increasing insulin resistance, which can lead to diabetes.
  2. Little to No Exercise (Cardio and Strength Training)
    • Contributes to obesity, high blood pressure, high blood sugar (glucose), and high LDL cholesterol levels.
    • Leads to lethargy, poor sleep, bad eating habits, unmitigated stress, and poor mental health.
    • Lowers bone density, muscle mass, and strength, which increase the chances of devastating falls and injuries.
  3. Smoking, Nicotine, and Alcohol Use
    • Damages organs like the lungs, liver, and kidneys, which significantly increases the risk of many cancers.
    • Damages and constricts blood vessels and raises blood pressure, which increases the risk of stroke.
    • Raises blood sugar (glucose) and A1C to toxic levels by reducing insulin sensitivity, which increases the risk of getting diabetes.
    • Raises LDL cholesterol to toxic levels by introducing harmful chemicals that interfere with the metabolism of LDL cholesterol, which accelerates plaque buildup in arteries, and increases the risk of a heart attack.
    • Alcohol ruins diets by creating intense hunger cravings for unhealthy food. Heavy drinking (over 14 drinks a week for men, 7 a week for women) significantly increases dementia risk, particularly early-onset dementia by 50-100%! Chronic heavy drinking can lead to brain damage and accelerate cognitive decline.
    • Catastrophic to sleep quality.
  4. High Blood Pressure (Hypertension)
    • The leading cause of stroke and a significant risk factor for dementia.
    • Damages several critical organs such as the heart, kidneys, liver, pancreas, and brain.
    • Damages blood vessels, which increases the chances of blockages and ruptures.
  5. Obesity and Being Overweight
    • Excess body weight is one of the leading causes of nearly all cancers, and a significant risk factor for developing diabetes, high blood pressure, and cardiovascular disease.
    • Dramatically increases the likelihood you're creating dangerous visceral fat and organ fat (liver, pancreas, etc.), which release inflammatory substances and hormones that can lead to deadly consequences.
    • Obese parents are more than twice as likely to have children with neurodevelopmental disorders (i.e. autism), metabolic syndrome, and behavioral challenges.
    • Leads to arthritis and severe back pain.
    • Reduces testosterone levels and increases estrogen levels.
Top 5 Solutions:
  1. Eat Better, Eat Less
    • Try eating fewer calories in general because eating less is one of the true panaceas for dramatically improving your health.
    • Try eating less refined carbohydrates, sugar, sodium, and saturated fats, which tend to be the biggest drivers of bad LDL cholesterol, triglycerides, and blood sugar (glucose).
    • Try eating more protein, soluble fiber, and unsaturated fats — like omega-3 fatty acids — to feel fuller faster, and for longer.
    • Thirst can often feel like hunger. So try drinking plenty of water before, during, and after a meal to help feel fuller faster. Try adding ice to the water to make it much more delicious and less boring.
    • For LDL cholesterol, strive for 70 mg/dL or less. For fasting blood sugar (glucose), strive for 70-80 mg/dL.
  2. Exercise a Little Each Day
    • 30 minutes a day is all you need.
    • Add strength training to your routine to build muscle strength, improve bone health, boost metabolism, and manage weight. And look more attractive! Aim for at least one 30-minute workout per week, and 6 sets per muscle group (e.g. chest).
    • Add walking to your routine for easy, light cardio, or find alternatives you prefer. Easy cardio strengthens your heart, improves lung capacity, and helps relieve stress, anxiety, and depression. Easy cardio can also improve digestion, relieve bloating, and strengthen the immune system. Aim for at least 30 minutes a day.
    • Add running to your routine for hard, intense cardio, or find alternatives you prefer. Hard cardio significantly boosts metabolic rate and provides a major reduction in cardiovascular disease and cancer risk. Aim for at least 10 minutes a day.
  3. Reduce Smoking, Nicotine, and Alcohol Use
    • For an energy boost, try drinking a low/no sugar caffeinated beverage or a glass of cold water. Try a cold plunge or shower. Try an exercise snack like taking a short walk or 10 body squats. Try a protein and fiber snack like almonds, pistachios, or cashews.
    • For anxiety relief, try eating a small meal, going on a walk, listening to some good music, napping, taking a hot shower, or talking through your anxiety with someone you trust.
  4. Lower Blood Pressure
    • Try reducing weight and body fat percentage.
    • Try reducing sodium and alcohol intake. Try staying hydrated and consuming enough potassium and magnesium.
    • Simply eating more protein and soluble fiber and less carbohydrates can substantially lower blood pressure.
    • Try exercising with an emphasis on intense running intervals and isometric holds like planks, wall sits, or yoga positions.
    • Try getting sufficient sleep and managing stress through healthy coping strategies (see Principle 6).
    • Try striving for 120/80 blood pressure or better.
  5. Lose Excess Weight
    • Losing even a little weight can dramatically reduce the chances of getting cardiovascular disease or cancer, as well as reduce blood pressure and improve insulin sensitivity. Losing weight can decrease toxic excess blood sugar (glucose), triglycerides, organ fat, and visceral fat.
    • Try eating foods that help you feel fuller faster, like protein and soluble fiber.
    • Try drinking plenty of fluids before, during, and after a meal to also help feel fuller faster.
    • Try lowering your daily caloric intake just a little.
    • Strive for 10% body fat for men, and 20% body fat or less for women (that's roughly a 25 BMI for men, and a 21 BMI for women).
2. Cancer (Lung, Colorectal, Pancreatic, etc.)

Cancer cells rely heavily on blood sugar (glucose) for energy and growth. Carbohydrates — especially sugars — convert quickly into glucose, which can rapidly accelerate cancer growth and spread.

Up to 40% of cancers can be prevented by stopping smoking, reducing alcohol intake, lowering weight, exercising, getting good sleep, and eating healthier foods. That's at least 4 million lives that could be saved, every single year.

The more you exercise, the lower your risk of cancer.
However, the more you exercise, the more energy you deplete for family, friends, and work. Find what works for you.

The closer you get to 10% body fat for men or 20% body fat or less for women, the lower your risk of cancer.

Plastics

Unfiltered tap water and heating plastics that contain your drinks or food are the two biggest consumption sources of toxic chemicals (BPA, PFAS, etc.) and microplastics.
Heating plastics drastically speeds up its breakdown into microplastics, which then leak into your food and drinks.

So try using a water filter, especially a reverse osmosis filter if you can afford it or even a pitcher water filter to help remove these toxic chemicals and microplastics. Pitcher water filters are fantastic and only cost around $50 for a great model.
Also, try to avoid plastic utensils or containers to make, handle, or store your food and drinks. Try using materials like glass, ceramic, metal, wood, or silicone.
This may be challenging to do, but even a small reduction in toxic chemical and microplastic exposure may provide serious health benefits.

But what if we've already consumed toxins and microplastics? Are we out of luck? Absolutely not. The two most effective ways to remove microplastics and toxins from your body is to sweat from heating up your body, as well as to consume soluble and insoluble fiber. You can sweat through exercise, sauna, or whichever method you prefer. Great sources of fiber include beans, fruit, vegetables, and supplements.

Oral Health

The consequences of unhealthy teeth, gums, and tongue are:

  1. Gum disease, tooth loss, and oral infections that can spread to your brain, which can be deadly.
  2. Higher likelihood of developing cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and even Alzheimer's disease.
  3. Bad breath and worse relationships because people tend to perceive you as irresponsible and careless.

Consider a dental checkup every 6 months, but if that's too costly, try to go at least once a year, or every other year at worst.

Brushing Your Teeth

The main goal for brushing and flossing your teeth is to remove plaque (dangerous bacteria) and decaying food from your mouth. Why? Because this decaying food supercharges the growth of dangerous plaque bacteria that drive disease and cancer.

So try to brush your teeth and tongue twice a day for at least 2 minutes each time, such as before bed and in the morning. That can seem daunting, but remember, you only need to use toothpaste for one of those times. You can just use water — the universal solvent — paired with brushing (friction) for the other time.

But even that can be challenging. So try to brush your teeth and tongue at least once a day with water, which can still produce fantastic results.

Flossing

While brushing your teeth will remove the majority of plaque bacteria and decaying food from your mouth, there will still be some stuck between your teeth. This insidious amount of plaque bacteria and decaying food can still drive disease and cancer, and lead to gum disease, tooth loss, and an increased risk of heart disease due to chronic oral inflammation. So try to floss at least once a week or more, and when food gets stuck in your teeth.

Skin Health

Lifestyle factors can significantly improve skin health and provide the biggest bang for your buck. Outside of skin issues driven by genetics (e.g., psoriasis, ichthyosis) or environment (e.g., pollution, UV exposure), by far the best ways to improve skin health and radiance are to sleep better, eat better, manage stress better, and exercise more.

Skin Cancer

The most important thing to keep in mind is change. If you have a spot on your body that looks weird but has looked weird and roughly the same size and color for years, it's probably not cancer. However, if you see a newer spot on your body that's changing size or color, or even an older spot that you've had for years but is starting to change size or color, then that needs to fire your alarm bells. Let your doctor know immediately so he or she can perform crucial tests that can save your life.
